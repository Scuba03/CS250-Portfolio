# CS 250 Portfolio – Software Development Lifecycle

This repository contains my final project artifact from CS 250: Software Development Lifecycle at Southern New Hampshire University. The artifact included is a Sprint Review and Retrospective written from the perspective of a Scrum Master on a development team building an application for SNHU Travel, a travel agency looking to expand its client base using innovative software tools.

---

## Artifact

**Sprint Review and Retrospective** – A reflection on the SNHU Travel project that covers how Scrum roles contributed to the project's success, how user stories were completed through the Agile process, how the team handled scope changes and interruptions, and an evaluation of the Scrum-Agile approach overall.

---

## Essential Questions

**How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating user stories help with this?**

Interpreting user needs means looking past what someone says they want and understanding what problem they are actually trying to solve. During the SNHU Travel project, I learned that writing requirements as user stories made a real difference in how the team approached their work. Instead of thinking about features in technical terms, stories like "as a traveler I want to filter search results by price range so that I only see options within my budget" kept the focus on the person using the application. That framing made acceptance criteria easier to define and helped the team build things that genuinely served the user rather than just checking a box. When a feature did not meet expectations after a sprint, the story format also made it easier to refine and resubmit it with clearer criteria the next time around.

**How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?**

I approach development by breaking work into smaller pieces and building incrementally rather than trying to plan everything perfectly before writing a single line of code. The SNHU Travel project reinforced for me that requirements change and that a process built around that reality produces better outcomes than one that treats change as a failure. Going forward I want to keep using sprint planning to set focused, achievable goals, daily standups to stay accountable and catch blockers early, and retrospectives to continuously improve the way I work. The retrospective is the practice I want to hold onto most because it creates a habit of honest reflection that makes every project a little better than the last one.

**What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?**

Being a good team member means making it easier for everyone around you to do their best work. In a Scrum environment that looks like showing up to standups prepared, communicating problems early instead of hoping they resolve themselves, and writing clear user stories so the people building them do not have to guess at the intent. It also means being willing to step outside your specific role when the team needs it. During the SNHU Travel project the most effective moments were when team members with different skill sets worked together on a problem rather than waiting to hand it off in sequence. Good collaboration is not just about being pleasant to work with. It is about taking shared ownership of the outcome.
